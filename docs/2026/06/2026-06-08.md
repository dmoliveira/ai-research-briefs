<h2 id="executive-summary">Executive Summary</h2>

This week is less about a single new model breakthrough and more about the systems boundary around agents. <em>Agent Memory</em> and <em>EMBER</em> are the clearest read-first papers because they turn persistent memory from a vague product feature into measurable write-path, read-path, grounding, and cost tradeoffs. Google Research's agentic RAG post points in the same direction from a product stack: complex enterprise questions need planning, fanout, sufficient-context checks, and iterative retrieval rather than one-shot chunk lookup.

The second major theme is credit assignment for long-horizon agents and reasoning models. <em>When Denser Credit Is Not Enough</em> argues that step-level rewards can amplify lucky actions unless calibrated by evidence and variance. <em>RREDCoT</em> tackles delayed rewards in chain-of-thought training with segment-level reward redistribution. <em>EDIT</em> applies similar localization pressure to rubric-faithful grading, where the important failure is not just the final score but the unsupported grading step that moved the model's belief.

The systems papers matter because the agent workloads are getting longer. <em>Vortex</em>, <em>You Only Index Once</em>, and <em>QCFuse</em> all target the serving bottlenecks that appear when agents reason, retrieve, and decode for many turns. Google's Gemma 4 QAT and Gemma 4 12B releases are product announcements, but they are relevant because they push multimodal and agentic workloads back toward laptops, edge devices, and local developer loops.

Finally, code, math, and science remain strong applied domains for agent design. <em>Code2LoRA</em> proposes generated repository adapters as an alternative to stuffing repo context into every prompt. <em>Goedel-Architect</em> uses blueprint graphs to organize formal theorem proving. Anthropic's chemistry note is small but concrete: it reports NMR prediction and inverse-elucidation tests where a general model competes with specialized chemistry software on a narrow benchmark.

<h2 id="key-trends">Key Trends</h2>

<div class="brief-trend-grid">
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Agent memory is becoming a systems problem.</strong></p>
    <p>The relevant metrics are no longer just recall or answer quality; construction cost, update timing, evidence survival, retrieval cost, and grounding are first-class design variables.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>RAG is moving from retrieval to control loops.</strong></p>
    <p>Agentic RAG, SARDI, QCFuse, and ProSPy all treat evidence gathering as iterative, query-aware, and system-dependent rather than as a fixed vector search call.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Credit assignment is the bottleneck for long tasks.</strong></p>
    <p>Several papers attack delayed, noisy, or mislocalized reward signals in agent training, reasoning traces, and grading workflows.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Local and efficient inference is back in focus.</strong></p>
    <p>Sparse attention, cache fusion, QAT, and mid-sized multimodal models aim to make long agent sessions affordable outside frontier hosted stacks.</p>
  </div>
