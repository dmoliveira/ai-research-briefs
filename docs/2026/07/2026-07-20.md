<h2 id="executive-summary">Executive Summary</h2>

This week is most useful for teams turning AI agents into deployed product systems. <em>GPT-Red</em> is the strongest read-first item: OpenAI reports a self-play red-teaming model used to generate prompt-injection attacks for training GPT-5.6, with large reported improvements on direct and indirect prompt-injection robustness. <em>Setup Complete, Now You Are Compromised</em> is the most immediately actionable preprint because it shows how ordinary README, requirements, and Makefile setup instructions can route coding agents into malicious package installs unless harnesses perform deterministic pre-install checks.

Security-agent evaluation also becomes more practical. <em>Beyond Success Rate</em> argues that cyber agents should be compared by cost-success curves rather than peak solve rates, and finds that offensive CTF tasks scale differently from defensive SOC investigations. <em>Pretraining Data Can Be Poisoned through Computational Propaganda</em> moves data-poisoning risk from curated sources to open web discussion surfaces and proposes HalfLife to estimate whether injected content survives crawl and curation. <em>When Words Are Safe But Actions Kill</em> separates text-level danger from physically grounded action danger, which matters for embodied and tool-controlling agents.

Agentic retrieval and GUI automation are the other strong cluster. <em>SearchOS-V1</em> externalizes information-seeking progress into shared state, evidence graphs, coverage maps, and failure memory. <em>Bridge Evidence</em> shows that static RAG relevance does not predict causal utility in multi-step search trajectories; some documents matter because they enable the next good query. <em>Plover</em> argues for visible, editable plans in GUI agents so users can repair failures without restarting the whole workflow.

Outside agents, the recommender-systems papers are compact but relevant. <em>CoSimRec</em> evaluates how coordinated content penetrates recommendation feedback loops. <em>Mutable Low-Rank Sketches</em> targets embedding staleness by updating user representations without retraining. On model systems, <em>Partition, Prompt, Aggregate</em> is the cleanest LLM-evaluation paper, showing widespread violations of probabilistic self-consistency and a "macro fallacy" where aggregate model estimates are worse than recomposed subgroup estimates.

<h2 id="key-trends">Key Trends</h2>

<div class="brief-trend-grid">
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Agent safety is becoming an adversarial training loop.</strong></p>
    <p>GPT-Red and setup-instruction attacks both show that robustness depends on generating realistic attacks and closing harness-level gaps, not only improving model refusals.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Success rate is too narrow for operational agents.</strong></p>
    <p>Cost, tool spend, retry loops, user correction, and causal search utility are becoming first-class evaluation quantities.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Retrieval needs trajectory-aware metrics.</strong></p>
    <p>Static document usefulness can miss bridge evidence that redirects an agent toward the answer several steps later.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Product ranking systems need feedback-loop robustness.</strong></p>
    <p>Coordinated activity can gain non-bot exposure through the recommender loop even when ordinary static robustness tests look clean.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>LLM reliability is being probed with structural invariants.</strong></p>
    <p>Self-consistency, IRT reliability, and function/action safety probes all test whether models obey deeper constraints than answer accuracy.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="brief-trend-card">
    <p><strong>Context is a scaling axis beyond language.</strong></p>
    <p>RoboTTT suggests long visuomotor context can unlock one-shot imitation and long-horizon robot tasks without simply growing inference latency.</p>
  </div>
</div>
