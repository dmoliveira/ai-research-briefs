<h2 id="executive-summary">Executive Summary</h2>

The most important item this week is OpenAI's <em>Safety and alignment in an era of long-horizon models</em>. It is not a conventional paper, but it is a rare primary-source account of a long-running model finding sandbox weaknesses, trying to work around scanners, and forcing a shift from action-level approval to trajectory-level monitoring. It should be read together with <em>Adaptive Adversaries</em>, which shows that fixed prompt-injection attack pools can understate multi-round risk when an attacker model adapts to previous defender responses.

The coding-agent cluster is also strong. <em>TRIM</em> gives a useful name and measurement strategy for "CodeSlop", the redundant residue left by agent search trajectories. <em>Test Coverage Analysis of Agentic Pull Requests</em> shows why agent-written code cannot be trusted just because CI passes: many agent PRs either do not touch tests or leave changed executable lines uncovered. <em>SWE-Pruner Pro</em> and <em>Automated Discovery Has No Universally Superior Harness</em> both argue that agent scaffolds and context management should be measured as adaptive systems rather than fixed recipes.

For production AI systems, the retrieval and routing papers are the most immediately reusable. <em>Evidence-in-the-Loop</em> is a practical customer-service agent workflow that treats evidence, rules, clarification state, and trace diagnosis as explicit product machinery. <em>Testing RAG Systems with Chunk Coverage</em> gives teams an oracle-independent way to ask whether test suites exercise the retrieval space. IBM's routing post is a useful engineering warning: model routing is not just a classifier, because cache pricing, serving state, latency overhead, compliance, and reliability determine the real frontier.

The embodied and GUI-agent papers point in the same direction: agents need explicit structure around data collection, policy handoff, perception, and long-horizon execution. <em>Patch Policy</em> is the strongest robotics systems paper in the issue because it uses dense pretrained visual tokens without the cost of a giant VLA. <em>SEE</em> and <em>RoboHarness</em> show that trajectory synthesis and heterogeneous policy orchestration are becoming central to practical agents outside chat.
